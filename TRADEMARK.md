# DeckTrail trademark policy

**Version 1.0.**

> Modelled on the published trademark policies of Mastodon, Grafana, Nextcloud, MinIO, the Linux
> Foundation, Mozilla and Debian. It is not legal advice.

The DeckTrail software is free. The DeckTrail **name** is not part of that grant.

This is normal, and every large open source project does it. The AGPL is a copyright licence:
it grants no rights in our trademarks, and it never purported to. AGPL-3.0 Section 7(e) exists
to confirm that declining to grant trademark rights is compatible with the licence.

**This policy does not add a term to the AGPL and takes nothing back from it.** It stands on
trademark law, which is separate from the licence and applies whether or not either document
exists. Nothing here affects your rights to the software.

## The marks

- **DeckTrail**, the name and logo.
- **OrbitQube**, the name and logo.

This policy covers our marks whether registered or unregistered. They are **unregistered**, so we
mark them ™ and not ®. If that changes, this policy changes with it.

## What you can do without asking

This is most things. We mean that.

- **Run DeckTrail**, modified or not, for anything, including commercially. That is the AGPL,
  and this policy takes nothing back from it.
- **Fork it and ship your fork**, under your own name. You need no permission from us.
- **Make true factual statements using our names.** Your fork is "based on DeckTrail",
  "compatible with DeckTrail", "derived from DeckTrail". This is nominative fair use, and it is
  yours by law rather than by our leave. Fair use does not stretch to stating or implying that
  we produce, endorse, or support your product.
- **Remove the "Made with DeckTrail by OrbitQube" mark from decks you render.** See
  `ATTRIBUTION.md`. We ask you to keep it. We do not require it, and you need no permission.
- **Write about DeckTrail**: articles, talks, tutorials, comparisons, reviews, criticism.
  Including unfavourable criticism. Especially that.
- **Redistribute DeckTrail unmodified** under its own name.

## What we ask you to ask about

- **Naming a modified version "DeckTrail"**, or a name close enough to be mistaken for it. Fork
  it, change it, ship it, but give it your own name. This is the core of the policy: when
  someone installs a thing called DeckTrail, they should get the thing we maintain. Because we
  publish a Docker image, please also rename the image and any binaries you modify.
- **Using the DeckTrail or OrbitQube logo** as the logo of your product or service.
- **Implying endorsement, affiliation, or partnership** with OrbitQube where none exists.
- **Naming a social media account** beginning with our marks, or a near variant of them.
- **Merchandise** carrying the marks.
- **Offering a commercial hosted service under the DeckTrail name.**

## What is not up for discussion

Not an ask, because there is nothing here we could sensibly say yes to:

- **Registering our marks**, or confusingly similar ones, as your trademarks or domain names.

## Using the logo

The DeckTrail logo is the switchback mark: a single gradient stroke that reads as a trail and the
stepped edge of a deck at once. Keep it as issued. Do not recolour it outside its palette, change
its proportions, distort it, rotate it, add effects, or set the wordmark in another typeface. Give
it clear space of at least the height of its own nodes on every side, and do not place it below
16 pixels, where it stops being legible. These rules protect the mark; they do not grant you any
right to use it, which is covered above.

## Attribution

When you refer to the marks in a way that needs a notice:

> "DeckTrail is a trademark of OrbitQube."

## How to ask

Open a request on the project's issue tracker, or email `info@orbitqube.com`. A short
description of what you want to do is enough. We are not looking for reasons to say no, and we
aim to answer within ten working days.

If we grant you permission and you then use the marks outside what we agreed, we may withdraw
that permission, and you would need to stop using them.

## Why we bother

**So the name keeps meaning something.** The product's whole pitch is that a consultant can
hand a deck to a client without losing track of it. If anyone can ship anything called
DeckTrail, the name stops carrying information, and the person harmed is the consultant who
trusted it.

**And because granting permission carries a duty.** Trademark law lets a mark be lost if its
owner licenses it out and then exercises no control over what ships under it. That is naked
licensing. It is why we say no to a small number of things rather than to nothing at all: if we
ever grant permission to use the name, we have to care what happens next.

It is **not** why we say no to forks. A fork is not our licensee and needs nothing from us, so
nothing a fork does puts the mark at risk. An earlier draft of this file said the opposite. It
claimed an open source project is "structurally exposed" to naked licensing because its name
travels with every fork, which inverts the doctrine: naked licensing requires a licence
relationship, and we grant none. The exposure runs the other way, through the permissions we
grant, which is the one place the earlier draft was not looking.

We would rather use this power narrowly and predictably than hold it in reserve. If this policy
is ever used to suppress a fork, a competitor, or criticism, we have misused it, and you should
say so loudly.

We will update it as the project grows, and the version at the top will say so.
